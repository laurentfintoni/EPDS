The internet and personal computers changed the world. Did they have an impact on the make up of art collections?

To answer this question we decided to look at two collections: MoMA, representing the more traditional art world, and Rhizome, a younger, smaller collection dedicated to net art, the kind of work made possible by the internet and personal computers.

MoMA's collection features different periods and movements, mainly focused on the 19th and 20th century. Rhizome is only focused on net art and spans the past 40 years.

So how can we answer our research question? Well there are two primary variables in our data - gender and nationality - so let's use these as lenses for investigation.

Gender representation in traditional & net art institutions

Gender is a useful concept to get a simple understanding of whether or not who is included in collections has changed.

But gender is not just a binary. In art, artists will sometimes work as collectives or collaborate together. This simple act of coming together gives us another dimension to break from the male/female binary.

Gender Representation in Rhizome and MoMA (sampled)

Rhizome has a higher percentage of female artists, MoMA has a lower percetange of male but higher collectives. Both have very low NB representation

It looks like collectives are more common in the years after the internet, but how are the different genders represented within them?

Who Takes Part in Collectives & Collaborations?

>MoMA features primarily male collectives and collaborations, while Rhizome has more mixed gender collectives. However MoMA has one collective/collaboration w/ a NB artist.

So what happens if we look at the MoMA dataset in full, but using 1983 - the year that the TCP/IP protocol became widely available as well as the year that Rhizome's first artwork was created - as a pivot date?

Gender Representation in MoMA before and after the Internet

correct size embeds

After 1983 the MoMA collection appears to be evening out in gender representation with more women, more collectives, and the appearance of the first non binary artists.

Oh, maybe there was a change. But! MoMA has 8 different departments and they're not all related to computers and the internet. So we selected four departments that are more closely aligned to net art: Photography, Media & Performance, Films, and Drawing & Print.

Gender Representation in MoMA departments before and after the Internet

three of the four departments show a difference in gender representation, with males decreasing and women increasing - Photography, Film and Drawings & Prints. Media & performance, which as we'll see is most closely aligned with Rhizome's net art, increased in overall size with more men, women, and collaborations as well as the only 3 artists who identify as non binary in the entire MoMA collection.

Rhizome doesn't have any department info, so we used the text available on the website - including artist statements and descriptions written by the institution - to allocate artworks to equivalent departments. The result is three overall departments that capture the various types of artworks we're most interested in:

  1. Still Images
  2. Moving Images
  3. Media & Performance (including Net Art)

Gender Representation across departments and institutions

The main observation here is that Rhizome appears to acquire more evenly across all its departments, while MoMA is acquiring most in the still images departments and least in moving images. Not sure what this says tbh!

Observation no2: The internet revolution didn't change centuries of gender imbalance but it seems to promote better gender balance in collections. However the representation of non binary people remains an issue with only 3 artists in MoMA's collection and 1 in Rhizome.
